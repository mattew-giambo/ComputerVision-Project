## 1. Introduction and Problem Statement

Modern deep generative models can easily produce highly realistic synthetic images. While these technologies are useful for creative work, they make it difficult to trust visual information. Distinguishing a real photograph from a synthetically generated one has become a major challenge for computer vision systems.

In real-world scenarios, this detection is even harder because images rarely remain in their original, clean state. When an image is shared online, it undergoes post-processing transformations. For example, social media platforms resize and compress files (JPEG compression), and physical sharing involves printing and re-photographing or scanning (re-digitalisation). These operations destroy the subtle high-frequency patterns and camera sensor noises that standard detectors rely on.

This project solves two complementary tasks simultaneously using a single model:
* **Task 1 — AI Detection:** Classify an image as either *real* (taken by a physical camera) or *ai* (generated by an AI model). This is a binary classification problem.
* **Task 2 — Post-Processing Classification:** Identify the transformation applied to the image: *original* (no processing), *redigital* (printed and re-scanned), or *transfer* (compressed through internet channels). This is a three-class classification problem.

We approach this as a **Multi-Task Learning (MTL)** problem. The core idea is that the two tasks are deeply related: they share a common feature space. Identifying the post-processing history helps the model isolate and remove transformation noise, which makes the AI detection head more robust. By learning both tasks jointly, the model acts as its own regulariser, improving generalisation under real-world image degradation.

## 2. State of the Art

### 2.1 AI-Generated Image Detection
Early detection methods trained standard convolutional networks (like ResNet-50) to classify images as real or fake in the spatial domain. To make these models robust to real-world alterations, researchers applied data augmentation during training, such as Gaussian blurring and JPEG compression. This acted as a regulariser, forcing the network to look at global structures rather than fragile pixel details.

However, generative models leave unique **spectral fingerprints** in the frequency domain. As studied in the course, convolutional upsampling (transposed convolutions) introduces periodic artifacts and grid-like patterns in the Fourier magnitude spectrum. Computing the 2D Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) allows these fingerprints to be isolated, providing a robust signal for synthetic image detection.

Recently, diffusion models have made detection harder. Because their iterative generation process is different from GANs, they do not produce the same periodic upsampling artifacts. This has pushed researchers to combine spatial texture, 3D geometry (RGB-D representations), and frequency-domain statistics to capture multiple distinct artifacts.

### 2.2 Post-Processing Robustness
Image forensic methods are often fragile when images undergo post-processing. Lossy JPEG compression, resizing, and printing wash away high-frequency camera noise patterns. 

To combat this, media forensics has explored high-pass filtering (such as Laplacian operators and band-pass filters) to isolate noise residuals while removing semantic contents. In our framework, we use a dedicated frequency branch (`FourierNet`) to process both magnitude and phase spectra. This helps the network separate the noise patterns introduced by transformations (such as scanner grid patterns or printer textures) from the original image details.

### 2.3 Multi-Task and Multi-Modal Learning
Multi-task learning (MTL) improves generalisation by forcing a shared backbone to learn features that are useful for multiple objectives. 

Furthermore, combining different modalities (like RGB and depth) helps expose inconsistencies. As studied in the course's MDE and deepfake detection modules, synthetic images often contain **geometric inconsistencies** (like incorrect perspective or unnatural 3D shadows) that are invisible in flat RGB pixels. By extracting a depth map using a monocular depth model (such as Depth Anything V2) and feeding it as a separate channel alongside RGB and Fourier features, we create a robust multi-modal detector that resists post-processing.

## 3. Theoretical Background

This section describes the key computer vision concepts from the lectures that are directly applied in the project.

### 3.1 The Fourier Transform and Frequency-Domain Analysis
Jean-Baptiste Joseph Fourier proposed that any periodic signal can be reconstructed by adding together simple sine waves, each defined by:
* **Amplitude** ($A$): the strength of the wave.
* **Frequency** ($\omega$): the speed of oscillation.
* **Phase** ($\phi$): the starting position or shift of the wave.

For an image, the 2D **Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT)** converts pixel values from the spatial domain to the frequency domain:

$$F(u, v) = \sum_{x=0}^{M-1} \sum_{y=0}^{N-1} f(x, y) \cdot e^{-j 2\pi \left(\frac{ux}{M} + \frac{vy}{N}\right)}$$

The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) is the fast algorithm used to compute this transform. The output is complex-valued, which we decompose into:
1. **Magnitude Spectrum** $|F(u, v)| = \sqrt{R(u,v)^2 + I(u,v)^2}$: represents the energy of each frequency.
2. **Phase Spectrum** $\angle F(u, v) = \arctan(I(u,v)/R(u,v))$: represents the spatial alignment of the waves.

In class, we discussed a classic experiment where the phase of a cheetah image is combined with the magnitude of a zebra image. The reconstructed image shows a cheetah with zebra-like textures. Conversely, combining the zebra's phase with the cheetah's magnitude depicts a zebra. This proves a fundamental rule: **the phase spectrum carries the structural details (edges and shapes) of the image, while the magnitude spectrum carries the frequency energy**. Therefore, our model uses both magnitude and phase to capture both structure and spectral noise.

Additionally, the **Convolution Theorem** states that convolution in the spatial domain corresponds to multiplication in the frequency domain:

$$\mathcal{F}[g * h] = \mathcal{F}[g] \cdot \mathcal{F}[h]$$

This means spatial filtering is equivalent to frequency weighting, showing how post-processing filters directly alter the frequency spectrum.

### 3.2 Convolutional Neural Networks for Image Recognition
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) learn hierarchical visual features. The layers consist of:
* **Convolutional layers**: apply local filters to extract spatial patterns.
* **Batch Normalisation (BN)**: normalises activations to stabilise and accelerate training.
* **Non-linear Activation**: ReLU or GELU functions that introduce non-linearity.
* **Max Pooling**: reduces spatial resolution to make features translation-invariant.
* **Global Average Pooling**: averages each feature map into a single value, reducing the spatial dimensions to 1×1 before the fully connected layers.

### 3.3 Monocular Depth Estimation and RGB-D Representations
Monocular Depth Estimation (MDE) predicts a dense depth map from a single RGB image. In the course, we studied **Depth Anything V2**, which uses a **Teacher-Student training framework with pseudo-labelling**:
1. A large **Teacher** model is trained on synthetic data.
2. The Teacher predicts depth maps (**pseudo-labels**) for a massive set of unlabeled real-world images.
3. A **Student** model is trained on these pseudo-labelled real-world images, learning to generalise across real-world environments.

In class, we saw how depth maps help detect deepfakes: generative models create realistic 2D faces but struggle with 3D consistency, leaving geometric flaws in the depth channel. We apply this concept to general images: the estimated depth map exposes spatial and geometric implausibilities in AI-generated scenes.

### 3.4 Deep Generative Models and Diffusion Models
Deep Generative Models learn the probability distribution of data ($p(x)$) to generate new samples. **Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM)** use two processes:
1. **Forward Process (Noising)**: Gradually adds Gaussian noise to a real image $x_0$ over $T$ steps according to a fixed schedule, turning it into pure noise $x_T$.
2. **Backward Process (Denoising)**: A neural network learns to reverse the noise addition step-by-step, starting from random noise $x_T$ and producing a clean image $x_0$.

Because diffusion models generate images via iterative denoising rather than convolutional upsampling, they do not show the typical grid-like frequency artifacts seen in GANs. This makes them harder to detect and requires the use of multi-modal features.

### 3.5 Multi-Task Learning as Regularisation
Multi-Task Learning (MTL) trains a single model to solve multiple tasks using a shared backbone. In our project, training the backbone on both AI detection and post-processing classification acts as **implicit regularisation**. It forces the network to learn robust visual-geometric-spectral representations that do not overfit to the noise of a single task.

## 4. Dataset (RRDataset & Pre-computed Depth)

### The RRDataset
We use the **RRDataset** (*Real/Redigital* dataset), a benchmark designed to test forensic robustness under real-world conditions. The dataset splits images along two independent axes:

* **AI Label (Task 1)**: `real` (taken by camera) and `ai` (synthetically generated).
* **Domain Label (Task 2)**: 
  * `original`: Unmodified digital images.
  * `redigital`: Images printed on paper and re-digitised using a scanner or camera.
  * `transfer`: Images uploaded and downloaded via social media platforms, introducing resizing and lossy JPEG compression.

This yields **six distinct subgroups**:
1. Original Real
2. Original AI
3. Redigital Real
4. Redigital AI
5. Transfer Real
6. Transfer AI

```
RRDataset_test/
├── original/
│   ├── ai/       ← AI-generated, unmodified
│   └── real/     ← Genuine, unmodified
├── redigital/
│   ├── ai/       ← AI-generated, printed-and-scanned
│   └── real/     ← Genuine, printed-and-scanned
└── transfer/
    ├── ai/       ← AI-generated, internet-transferred
    └── real/     ← Genuine, internet-transferred
```

### Dataset Balancing and Splits
To avoid dataset bias, we cap the dataset at **15,000 samples** and balance it using a uniform quota strategy. This ensures that each of the six subgroups contains exactly the same number of samples. The balanced dataset is split deterministically (random seed = 42) into:

* **Training Set (70%)**: ~10,500 samples.
* **Validation Set (15%)**: ~2,250 samples.
* **Test Set (15%)**: ~2,250 samples.

All models and baselines are trained and evaluated on these exact same splits.

### Depth Map Dataset
The folder `RRDataset_depth` contains pre-computed monocular depth maps for every image. To save GPU memory and training time, we pre-computed the depth maps **offline** using **Depth Anything V2 (Small)**. Each map is stored as a single-channel `.pt` tensor (shape `[1, 224, 224]`) normalised to `[0, 1]`.

## 5. Proposed Method — MultiTaskNet

### 5.1 Architecture Overview
`MultiTaskNet` is a hierarchical multi-modal and multi-task network. It extracts features from three parallel specialist pathways (RGB, Depth, and Fourier) and fuses them in stages before sending them to two separate classification heads.

```
                  Input Image (RGB) and Depth Map (D)
                                  │
         ┌────────────────────────┼────────────────────────┐
         │                        │                        │
         ▼                        ▼                        ▼
     [RGBNet]                 [DepthNet]              [FourierNet]
  (3-ch RGB input)       (1-ch Depth input)        (6-ch FFT input)
     [B, 128]                 [B, 128]                 [B, 128]
     [B, 64]                  [B, 64]                  [B, 64]
         │                        │                        │
         └───────── cat ──────────┘                        │
                 [B, 128]                                  │
           [Stage 2: fc_stage2]                            │
             Linear(128→64)                                │
             GELU + Dropout                                │
                 [B, 64]                                   │
                    │                                      │
                    └───────────────── cat ────────────────┘
                                    [B, 128]
                              [Stage 3: fc_stage3]
                                Linear(128→256)
                                GELU + Dropout
                                    [B, 256]
                                       │
                        ┌──────────────┴──────────────┐
                        ▼                             ▼
               [BinaryClassifier]           [TransformClassifier]
                Linear(256→128→2)            Linear(256→128→3)
                  (Task 1 Head)                (Task 2 Head)
```

### 5.2 Offline Depth Pre-Computation
Running MDE in real time during training is computationally heavy. Instead, we use `extract_depth_maps_offline()` once before training:
1. Load **Depth Anything V2 (Small)**.
2. Predict the depth map for every image under `RRDataset_test/`.
3. Interpolate the depth map to 224×224 and scale its values to `[0, 1]`.
4. Save the depth map as a `.pt` tensor under `RRDataset_depth/` using the same directory structure.

At training time, the dataset loads these `.pt` files directly at zero computational cost.

### 5.3 FourierNet Branch and Log-Magnitude Input
The Fourier branch processes the frequency representation of the RGB image:
1. **FourierNetInput**: Computes the 2D Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) of the 3-channel RGB image.
2. **Shift**: Shifts the low-frequency components to the center of the spectrum (`fftshift`).
3. **Magnitude & Phase**: Extracts the magnitude (`torch.abs`) and the phase (`torch.angle`).
4. **Log-Compression**: Applies `log(|X| + 1)` to the magnitude spectrum to reduce the dominance of the DC component and make spectral patterns visible at all frequency scales.
5. **Concat**: Concatenates magnitude and phase into a 6-channel tensor (shape `[B, 6, 224, 224]`).

This tensor is passed to a three-block CNN:
* Block 1: Conv(6→32, kernel=3, pad=1) + BN + ReLU + MaxPool(2x2)
* Block 2: Conv(32→64, kernel=3, pad=1) + BN + ReLU + MaxPool(2x2)
* Block 3: Conv(64→128, kernel=3, pad=1) + BN + ReLU + MaxPool(2x2)
* Global Pool: AdaptiveAvgPool2d(1, 1) -> flattening to `[B, 128]`
* Bottleneck: Linear(128→64) + ReLU + Dropout(0.3) -> 64-dimensional frequency embedding.

### 5.4 RGB and Depth Specialist Branches
* **RGBNet** processes the 3-channel RGB image to extract spatial textures.
* **DepthNet** processes the 1-channel depth map to extract 3D structural shapes.

They share the same network structure:
* Block 1: Conv(in_ch→32, kernel=3, pad=1) + BN + GELU + MaxPool(2x2)
* Block 2: Conv(32→64, kernel=3, pad=1) + BN + GELU + MaxPool(2x2)
* Block 3: Conv(64→128, kernel=3, pad=1) + BN + GELU + MaxPool(2x2)
* Global Pool: AdaptiveAvgPool2d(1, 1) -> flattening to `[B, 128]`
* Bottleneck: Linear(128→64) + GELU + Dropout(0.3) -> 64-dimensional embedding.

Note that `RGBNet` and `DepthNet` do **not** share weights. Keeping them separate prevents the networks from mixing color information with pure 3D geometry during the first stage.

### 5.5 Hierarchical Multi-Stage Fusion (RGB-D and Fourier)
We fuse the three 64-dimensional embeddings in two steps:

1. **Stage 2 — Intermediate Spatial Fusion (RGB ⊕ Depth)**:
   We concatenate the spatial embeddings: `torch.cat((x_rgb, x_depth), dim=1)` yielding a 128-dimensional vector. This is projected back to 64 dimensions using `fc_stage2` (Linear(128, 64) + GELU + Dropout(0.3)). Since both RGB and depth represent spatial pixel coordinates, early fusion allows the network to learn spatial relationships (e.g., whether a flat texture anomaly aligns with a geometric edge).
2. **Stage 3 — Late Frequency Fusion (Visual-Geometric ⊕ Fourier)**:
   We concatenate the visual-geometric embedding (64-dim) and the Fourier embedding (64-dim): `torch.cat((x_visual_geo, x_freq), dim=1)` yielding a 128-dimensional vector. This is projected to the final 256-dimensional representation using `fc_stage3` (Linear(128, 256) + GELU + Dropout(0.3)). We use late fusion here because the Fourier spectrum represents global frequency statistics and has no spatial coordinates. Merging it earlier would disrupt the spatial filters of the RGB and Depth CNNs.

### 5.6 Classification Heads
The 256-dimensional fused embedding is sent to two separate heads:
* **Binary Head (AI Detection)**: LayerNorm(256) → Linear(256, 128) → GELU → Dropout(0.4) → Linear(128, 2). Outputs logits for the classes `{real, ai}`.
* **Transform Head (Post-Processing Classification)**: LayerNorm(256) → Linear(256, 128) → GELU → Dropout(0.4) → Linear(128, 3). Outputs logits for the classes `{original, redigital, transfer}`.

### 5.7 Multi-Task Loss and weighting parameter Lambda
The network is optimized using a combined loss function:

$$\mathcal{L} = \lambda \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{AI}} + (1 - \lambda) \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{domain}}$$

Where:
* $\mathcal{L}_{\text{AI}}$ is the Cross-Entropy loss for Task 1.
* $\mathcal{L}_{\text{domain}}$ is the Cross-Entropy loss for Task 2.
* $\lambda \in [0, 1]$ is the scalar weighting parameter that controls the trade-off between the two tasks.

We trained 5 independent models with $\lambda \in \{0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9\}$ to locate the optimal balance on the Pareto frontier, and two unimodal baselines ($\lambda = 1.0$ and $\lambda = 0.0$).

## 6. Experimental Setup

* **Image Preprocessing**: Resized to 224×224 pixels. Standard ImageNet normalisation is applied to the RGB channel. Depth maps are normalized to `[0, 1]`.
* **Optimizer**: AdamW with a constant learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-4}$.
* **Batch Size**: 100.
* **Epochs**: Up to 50, with **Early Stopping** (patience = 5 epochs) monitoring validation loss.
* **Hardware & Acceleration**: Trained on a GPU using Mixed-Precision training (`torch.amp.GradScaler`) to reduce VRAM consumption and speed up training.

* The unimodal domain baseline ($\lambda = 0.0$) achieves 60.3% domain accuracy, but its AI detection accuracy is at chance level (~50.0%).
* The multi-task model at $\lambda = 0.5$ achieves **82.6% AI detection accuracy** (matching the unimodal model) and **59.5% domain classification accuracy** (matching the domain-only model).
* This provides scientific validation for multi-task learning: a single shared model can solve both tasks simultaneously without any degradation in performance, confirming that the two objectives provide mutual regularisation.

## 8. Model Selection and Best Configuration

Based on our evaluation, we select the model trained with **$\lambda = 0.5$** as the best configuration. Our choice is based on the following reasons:

1. **Maximum AI Detection Performance:** It achieves 82.6% accuracy and 0.826 macro-F1 on Task 1, which matches the peak performance of the highly focused $\lambda = 0.9$ model.
2. **Balanced Domain Classification:** It maintains a domain classification accuracy of 59.5%, which is very close to the unimodal baseline peak (60.3%).
3. **Generalisation and Robustness:** As shown in the per-domain breakdown, $\lambda = 0.5$ achieves the highest AI detection accuracies on original (82.93%) and internet-transferred (84.34%) images, and a highly competitive 80.61% on re-digitalised images.
4. **Parameter Efficiency:** It solves both tasks with a single set of shared backbone weights, saving half the parameters and training time compared to using two separate unimodal networks.

## 10. Conclusions and Future Work

### 10.1 Summary of Findings
We proposed and implemented `MultiTaskNet`, a multi-modal and multi-task architecture for detecting synthetic images and identifying post-processing alterations. By combining RGB textures, monocular depth geometry, and frequency spectra, our model is highly robust to post-processing. The evaluation shows that:
* The model trained with $\lambda = 0.5$ achieves the best performance, matching the accuracy of unimodal models on both tasks simultaneously.
* Re-digitalised (printed and scanned) images are the hardest domain for AI detection, but our multi-modal features keep the performance drop to a minimum (only 2.3% drop).
* Staged fusion (fusing RGB and depth early, and frequency late) successfully prevents global frequency statistics from disrupting spatial filters in the convolutional branches.

### 10.2 Limitations
* **Dataset Size:** Capping the dataset at 15,000 samples limits the variety of generative models and scanning devices the network can learn.
* **Depth Map Precision:** The depth maps are estimated offline using a monocular model (Depth Anything V2) rather than measured by physical sensors. For complex outdoor scenes, estimated depth can be noisy.
* **Domain Classification Ceiling:** The domain classification accuracy remains capped at ~60%, showing that the network struggles to separate subtle JPEG compression levels from clean digital images.

### 10.3 Future Work
* **VLM and Foundation Models:** Replacing our custom CNN encoders with pre-trained vision-language features or larger backbones (like ViT or DINOv2) could improve feature extraction.
* **Explainability (Grad-CAM):** Implementing Grad-CAM (studied in the course) would allow us to visualize which spatial regions and frequency bands the model focuses on for each task.
* **Dynamic Loss Weighting:** Using an automated loss weighting scheduler (like GradNorm) instead of a fixed $\lambda$ could improve convergence speed.
* **Adversarial Robustness:** Testing the model against adversarial attacks (images designed to trick AI detectors) would assess the system's readiness for real-world deployment.
